I.
The counterexample

XOR breaks a single perceptron.

A single neuron computes activation(w·x + b) — its boundary is one straight line. XOR's two diagonally opposite corners cannot be separated by any line. Stack layers with a non-linearity between them and the problem dissolves.

Single perceptron

One line, can't win.

Any straight line you pick will misclassify two of the four corners. Adding more inputs doesn't help — the boundary is still linear in those inputs. Width without depth is still flat.

MLP with ReLU

Two layers, solved.

A 2-hidden-unit MLP with ReLU bends the input space along two lines — their intersection carves XOR's checkerboard. Five layers of linear still collapse to one linear function; the non-linearity unlocks depth.

III.
The engine

Gradient descent. One knob matters most.

On a 1D parabola L(w) = (w-3)², gradient descent walks downhill. Too low a learning rate and training crawls; too high and it overshoots and diverges; Goldilocks and the loss bends smoothly to zero.

Two knobs

0.01 (crawl)1.80 (diverge)
-2.020.0

Minimum is at w = 3. Adam adapts this learning rate per parameter automatically — which is why lr=1e-3 with Adam is the safe default for tabular nets.

IV.
The template

Memorise these five lines.

Identical for an MLP, a CNN, or a transformer — only the model class and loss change. Every line in the rest of M3 lives inside this loop.

  optimizer.zero_grad()
  logits = model(X_batch)
  loss   = criterion(logits, y_batch)
  loss.backward()
  optimizer.step()
1
zero_grad() · clear stale gradients from the previous batch. Forget this and gradients accumulate; by step 10 you're taking a 10×-sized step in a stale direction.
2
model(X) · forward pass. PyTorch records every operation on the computation graph for autograd.
3
criterion(...) · compute the scalar loss. BCEWithLogitsLoss for binary; CrossEntropyLoss for multi-class.
4
loss.backward() · autograd walks the graph backwards, filling .grad on every parameter. The chain rule, done for you.
5
optimizer.step() · update each parameter using its .grad and the optimiser's rule (Adam by default).
V.
A checklist

Three checks before you blame the architecture.

Skip these and you'll burn an afternoon tuning hidden-layer width when the real bug was the learning rate.

1

Did it converge?

Train loss should fall, then plateau. Still falling sharply? You stopped too early. Bouncing wildly? lr too high. Flat from epoch 1? lr too low or data not reaching the model.

✕ "Trained 10 epochs, accuracy 0.50."✓ "Train loss bends, plateaus by epoch 30."
2

Is it overfitting?

Train falls while val bottoms out and rises — the widening gap is overfitting. Early-stop on best val, regularise, add data, or shrink the model.

✕ "Train 0.18, val 0.38 — train more!"✓ "Restore epoch 12; val bottomed there."
3

Is the lr right?

Sweep 1e-2, 1e-3, 1e-4 before tuning anything else. Healthy = fast drop, smooth bend. Jagged = too high. Flat = too low.

✕ "Default lr, three widths."✓ "lr sweep first; then width."

VI.
Check yourself

Nine questions from the field notebook.

Click to flip. Numbering matches lesson.md.

▸ Click to revealWhy doesn't a "bigger perceptron with more inputs" solve XOR?
A perceptron computes activation(w·x + b) — its boundary is one hyperplane. XOR (1s in opposite corners) cannot be separated by any line. Adding inputs keeps the boundary linear in those inputs. The fix is depth + a non-linearity, not width.
▸ Click to reveal5-layer MLP with identity activations — what happens?
The whole network collapses to a single linear function. Composing linear maps gives another linear map; 5 layers = 1 linear layer in expressive power. The non-linearity is what makes "deep" different from "wide-but-flat".
▸ Click to revealLR, MLP, GB all within 0.015 AUC. When does the MLP pull ahead?
When data is large (millions of rows), features have non-linear interactions feature engineering can't capture, or you need to ensemble with other neural models. The MLP's real edge appears in L08–L10, where inputs are pixels or tokens.
▸ Click to revealWhat does optimizer.zero_grad() do, and what breaks if you forget?
PyTorch accumulates gradients in .grad on each backward call. zero_grad() clears them. Forget it and each batch's gradient is added to all previous; by step 10 you're taking a 10×-sized step in a stale direction. Training diverges.
▸ Click to revealL(w) = (w-3)², start w=10. lr=1.5 vs lr=0.001?
lr=1.5: each step overshoots; iterates oscillate with growing amplitude; loss diverges. lr=0.001: every step is tiny; the path crawls toward 3 and may not arrive in your epoch budget. Sweet spot ~0.01–0.1; Adam removes most of this tuning.
▸ Click to revealRaw logits + BCELoss. Garbage outputs. Why?
BCELoss expects probabilities in [0, 1]. Raw logits are any real number; feeding them gives nonsensical, numerically unstable gradients. Fix: use BCEWithLogitsLoss, which fuses sigmoid + BCE for stability.
▸ Click to revealWrite the five lines of a PyTorch training step.
(1) optimizer.zero_grad()
(2) logits = model(X)
(3) loss = criterion(logits, y)
(4) loss.backward()
(5) optimizer.step()
▸ Click to revealTrain 0.18 still falling; val bottomed at 0.31 (ep 12), now 0.38. Healthy?
No — textbook overfitting. The widening gap means memorisation. Early-stop: restore epoch 12 weights. For better val loss try smaller model, more data, dropout/weight decay — not more epochs.
▸ Click to revealWhy call model.eval() before validation?
For a tiny MLP it doesn't change numbers — but it's a habit that prevents silent bugs the moment you add dropout or batch-norm. Dropout zeros activations in train mode; batch-norm uses batch stats in train, running stats in eval. Always wrap evaluation in model.eval() + with torch.no_grad():.
